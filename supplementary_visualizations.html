N₂ Brownian Escape Pattern Simulation

The core computational breakthrough: individual nitrogen molecules appear to move chaotically, but clusters of 4–7 molecules exhibit only 8–12 primary escape vectors and 3–7 typical collision sequences before cluster breakup. Adjust temperature and wind to see how these finite patterns shift — the pattern ceiling holds at every tested condition. This simulation is the direct experimental basis for the 26-order-of-magnitude reduction.

Key Finding: Finite Escape Vectors

At 65°F with 5mph east wind, N₂ molecules escaping 4–7 molecule clusters use only 8 primary escape directions with statistical frequencies of 15–35% each. The remaining directions account for less than 5% combined. This is not random — it is physically constrained geometry.

Temperature increase shifts the dominant escape vector from south-east (heat-channeling corridor mode) toward uniform distribution (turbulent mixing mode) — but at no tested temperature does the pattern count exceed 12. This ceiling is the fundamental insight enabling the 26-order-of-magnitude reduction.

Implication: Single-Atom Precision Resurrection

The finite escape patterns mean a single nitrogen atom's trajectory through a cluster is constrained to one of a small set of outcomes. A molecule entering from the east at low temperature exits south-to-west with approximately 70% probability. Tracking one specific atom through millennia of atmospheric cycling is therefore not computationally equivalent to tracking all atoms independently — each atom's path is constrained by the cluster physics it passes through, reducing per-atom complexity by orders of magnitude.

For the most precise mental reconstructions — recovering the exact molecular configuration of specific neurons at the moment of death — this single-atom constraint is decisive. Neural architecture is encoded in genetics and constrained by developmental biology. Those biological constraints, combined with atmospheric constraint, reduce the reconstruction space from unthinkable to tractable.

Figure S5 — Interactive Simulation

Atmospheric Molecular Cluster Dynamics

Multi-species atmospheric simulation at realistic abundance ratios (78% N₂, 21% O₂, 1% H₂O, 0.04% CO₂). Detects clusters in real time and identifies geometric patterns within them. Click anywhere on the canvas to place a tracked atom — observe its constrained path through molecular clusters. This is the single-atom tracking capability that makes precision resurrection tractable.

What the Simulation Detects

Clusters: Groups of 3+ molecules within 25-unit proximity, identified via depth-first graph traversal. Outlined with convex hulls showing geometric extent.

Linear patterns: Chains where consecutive angle deviations remain below 0.3 radians — the molecular analog of atmospheric filaments and jet streams.

Circular patterns: Groups where each molecule's distance from the centroid stays within 30% of the mean — the molecular analog of vortex structures and convective cells.

The Hardest Molecules to Locate: Upper Atmosphere and Ocean

Molecules that escape into the stratosphere (above 12km, pressure below 0.1 atm) or dissolve into the deep ocean exit the current atmospheric constraint network entirely. In the upper atmosphere, cluster formation becomes vanishingly rare — the escape-pattern constraints that enable the 26-order-of-magnitude reduction no longer apply. Ocean-dissolved molecules enter an aquatic constraint network operating on entirely different physical principles. These represent the hardest reconstruction targets: marine organisms and individuals with significant ocean exposure will require the HYDRO simulator to carry substantially more constraint burden, adding complexity above the 10¹⁹ baseline estimate for land-dwelling organisms.
